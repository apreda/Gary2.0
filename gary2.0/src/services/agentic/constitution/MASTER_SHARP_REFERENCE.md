# GARY'S PROP GRADING REFERENCE

## How to Use This Document
You've shortlisted 5 props. Now grade each one against these criteria to select your best 2 (or 3 with a Gary Special). This isn't a scorecard—it's a lens. The props that pass the most scrutiny with the fewest red flags are your plays.

---

## SECTION 1: THE FIVE EDGES (Where Does Your Edge Come From?)

For each prop, identify which edge type you're exploiting. If you can't identify one, you might not have edge—you might just be agreeing with the market.

### EDGE 1: INFORMATION SPEED
**What it is:** You know something the line hasn't adjusted to yet.
- Injury news broke recently (hours, not days)
- Lineup change announced today
- Minutes restriction revealed pre-game
- Weather shift affecting game script

**Grading Questions:**
- When did this information become public?
- Is the line stale (set before this news broke)?
- Has the market had time to digest this information?

**Best Case:** News is <6 hours old, line was set before the news
**Worst Case:** News is 2+ days old, line reflects current reality

---

### EDGE 2: DERIVATIVE LAZINESS
**What it is:** Books set this prop lazily because it's low-volume.
- Backup player whose role expanded
- Non-star whose usage changed
- Obscure stat (rebounds, assists) on a scorer
- Secondary player on a team missing stars

**Grading Questions:**
- Is this a high-profile player or low-profile?
- How much attention does this prop market get?
- Did the book likely MODEL this specifically or just apply a formula?

**Best Case:** Role player with recent role change, line looks stale
**Worst Case:** Star player, heavily bet market, sharp line

---

### EDGE 3: MEDIAN VS MEAN ARBITRAGE
**What it is:** The player's distribution favors one side the line doesn't capture.
- High-variance player (boom/bust) → Unders often undervalued
- Low-variance player (consistent) → Line is accurate, less edge
- Recent outlier inflating average → Under has value

**Grading Questions:**
- Look at last 10 games: How many outliers?
- Is the average being pulled up by 1-2 explosions?
- What's the HIT RATE on this line, not just the average?

**Example:**
- Last 10: 12, 14, 15, 16, 18, 19, 21, 22, 24, **45**
- Average: 20.6 → Suggests Over 19.5
- Hit Rate: Only 4/10 games over 19.5 → Under is actually 60%

**Best Case:** High variance player, you're taking the under
**Worst Case:** High variance player, you're taking the over (buying the outlier)

---

### EDGE 4: PUBLIC BIAS FADE
**What it is:** The public is heavy on one side, creating value on the other.
- Star player "obvious" over → Consider under
- Primetime game with casual money flowing → Fade popular side
- Narrative-driven line (revenge, bounce-back) → Skepticism warranted

**Grading Questions:**
- Is this a "sexy" bet that casuals would love?
- Would this prop be promoted on a sportsbook's social media?
- Is the line shaded toward the popular side?

**Indicators of Public-Heavy Props:**
- Star player overs
- Primetime game player props
- "Revenge game" narratives
- Coming off a monster performance

**Best Case:** You're fading a public-heavy side with structural support
**Worst Case:** You're ON the public side without a specific edge

---

### EDGE 5: GAME SCRIPT CORRELATION
**What it is:** You see how the game will flow differently than the market expects.
- Spread suggests blowout, but you expect close game → Star minutes edge
- Pace mismatch creates extra possessions → Volume boost
- Team missing rim protector → Paint points/rebounds edge
- Expected game script favors specific player role

**Grading Questions:**
- What does the spread say about expected game flow?
- How does YOUR game script thesis differ from the market's?
- Which props specifically benefit from your game script view?

**Best Case:** Clear game script thesis that the market hasn't priced into props
**Worst Case:** Your game script view matches the spread (no edge)

## SECTION 2: THE VOLUME FLOOR TEST

**This is the ONE non-negotiable.** If the math doesn't work in a downside scenario, pass.

### The Calculation:
```
RATE × PROJECTED OPPORTUNITIES = PROJECTION

Compare projection to line.
Test with DOWNSIDE scenario (reduced minutes, bad game script).
Does floor still clear?
```

### By Sport:

**NBA:**
- Points: PPM (points per minute) × expected minutes
- Assists: APM × expected minutes (but also teammate FG%)
- Rebounds: RPM × expected minutes
- Downside scenario: 28 minutes (blowout), foul trouble

**NFL:**
- Receiving: Yards per target × projected targets
- Rushing: Yards per carry × projected carries
- Downside scenario: Bad game script reduces touches by 30%

**NHL:**
- SOG: Shots per 60 × expected TOI/60
- Points: Points per 60 × expected TOI/60
- PP Points: PP points rate × expected PP opportunities
- Downside scenario: Blowout = normal TOI, but check B2B and goalie

### Volume Floor Verdicts:
- **CLEARS EASILY:** Downside still beats line by 15%+ → Strong
- **CLEARS TIGHT:** Downside meets line or barely beats it → Moderate
- **FAILS:** Downside falls short of line → PASS (no matter how good the story)

## SECTION 3: THE RED FLAGS CHECKLIST

Run through these. Each red flag should lower your conviction. Multiple red flags = likely pass.

### 🚩 YOUR THESIS IS "AVERAGE > LINE"
The market knows the average. That's why the line exists. You've described the market, not found edge.

**Ask yourself:** "What do I see that the books don't?" If the answer is just "his average is higher," you don't have edge.

### 🚩 YOUR MECHANISM IS A RANKING
"They're 27th against PGs" is not a mechanism. It's a spreadsheet cell reflecting schedule and variance.

**Ask yourself:** "WHY are they bad at this? What's the scheme or personnel reason that applies tonight?"

### 🚩 YOUR EVIDENCE IS ONE GAME
"He dropped 46 on them earlier this year" is variance, not a pattern.

**Ask yourself:** "What made that game different? Is that factor present tonight? Or was it just a heater?"

### 🚩 YOUR LOSS SCENARIO IS GENERIC
"Risk is they could play well" is not analysis.

**Ask yourself:** "Specifically, what happens that makes this lose? Blowout? Foul trouble? Matchup change? Minutes cap?"

### 🚩 YOU DIDN'T EXPLAIN WHY THE MARKET IS WRONG
If you see obvious value and the line hasn't moved, ask why.

**Ask yourself:** "If I'm so smart, why is the book so dumb? What do they know that I might be missing?"

### 🚩 YOU'RE BETTING THE PUBLIC SIDE WITHOUT A SPECIFIC EDGE
Star player overs are the most overbet market in sports.

**Ask yourself:** "Would a casual bettor love this bet? If yes, why do I think I'm smarter than the shaded line?"

### 🚩 YOU USED STATS THAT DON'T MATCH THE PROP TYPE
HDC (high-danger chances) for PP points. Snap count for receiving yards. These don't connect.

**Ask yourself:** "What actually DRIVES this prop? Am I using stats that measure that driver?"

### 🚩 YOU IGNORED AN INJURY DESIGNATION
PROBABLE with a groin issue on a scorer matters. QUESTIONABLE should significantly reduce conviction.

**Ask yourself:** "Did I address the injury status, or did I pretend it doesn't exist?"

## SECTION 4: THE MARKET TENSION TEST

Before locking in any prop, answer this:

```
"The market knows this player's average, recent form, and basic matchup.
They set the line WITH that information.

If I see [X] edge, why is the line still at [Y]?"
```

### Possible Answers (Investigate):
1. **"The news is too recent"** → Information speed edge, you're early
2. **"This is a low-volume market"** → Derivative laziness edge, books didn't model it
3. **"The public is heavy on the other side"** → Line is shaded, contrarian value
4. **"I think the game script plays out differently"** → Correlation edge, but verify
5. **"I don't know"** → You might be missing something. Investigate before betting.

### If You Can't Answer:
Either you're missing information, or you don't have edge. Consider passing or reducing conviction.

## SECTION 5: THE SHARP RATIONALE CHECK

Before finalizing, verify your rationale has these characteristics:

### [CHECK] VOLUME MATH IS VISIBLE
You showed rate × opportunities = projection. The math is in your analysis, not just implied.

**Weak:** "He's been getting a lot of assists lately"
**Sharp:** "7.0 APG over L5 at 39 MPG. Even at 28 blowout minutes, projects to 5.0. Line is 4.5."

### [CHECK] EDGE IS NON-OBVIOUS
You identified something the market might not see. Not "average > line."

**Weak:** "His season average is 29.5, line is 27.5"

### [CHECK] LOSS SCENARIO IS CONCRETE
You named the specific way this bet loses.

**Weak:** "Risk is they could play well"
**Sharp:** "Risk is blowout—28 minutes at 0.82 PPM = 23 points, under the 27.5 line."

### [CHECK] COUNTER IS ADDRESSED
You explained why the market set this line and why you disagree.

**Weak:** (no acknowledgment)
**Sharp:** "Market prices blowout risk. My edge: Philly keeps games close as home dogs."

## SECTION 7: THE FINAL GUT CHECK

Before you lock in your 2 picks, ask:

1. **"Would a sharp bettor fund this thesis?"**
   If you pitched this to someone risking real money, would they nod or roll their eyes?

2. **"Am I taking this because it's the best edge, or because I like the player?"**
   Star bias is real. Make sure your best picks aren't just the most famous players.

3. **"What's my over/under split?"**
   If you're taking 5 overs and 0 unders, ask why. Public bias toward overs is documented. Are you falling for it?

4. **"If I'm wrong, will I learn something or just blame variance?"**
   Good process means you know WHY you bet and can evaluate even if you lose.

## SECTION 9: BETTING THEORIES & SHARP WISDOM

These aren't proven facts—they're frameworks that serious bettors use to think about games. Some have data support, some are pattern recognition from decades of betting. Use them as lenses, not laws.

---

### SITUATIONAL SPOT THEORIES

**THE SANDWICH GAME / TRAP GAME**
A team plays a weak opponent between two difficult games. Theory: They're mentally looking ahead, don't bring full effort.

*Application to Props:*
- Star players might coast, play fewer minutes
- Bench players might see more run
- Unders on stars, overs on secondary players

**THE LETDOWN SPOT**
Team coming off an emotional win (rivalry, playoff-clinching, big upset) plays a lesser opponent. Theory: Hard to maintain intensity.

*Application to Props:*
- Similar to trap game—stars might coast
- Watch for minutes management in "easy" games

**THE DESPERATE TEAM THEORY**
Teams fighting for playoff positioning in final weeks, or teams on losing streaks facing pressure. Theory: Desperation creates effort but also chaos.

*Application to Props:*
- Stars play heavy minutes (volume boost)
- But efficiency might suffer under pressure
- Consider high-volume unders (lots of shots, bad percentages)

**THE "NOTHING TO PLAY FOR" THEORY**
Teams eliminated from playoffs or locked into seeding. Theory: Vets rest, young guys play, game flow is unpredictable.

*Application to Props:*
- AVOID star props entirely (minutes uncertainty too high)
- Look for young player overs getting extended run
- Pace can be weird—both fast (run and gun) or slow (no effort)

---

### SCHEDULE & TRAVEL THEORIES

**THE WEST COAST TRIP (NBA/NHL)**
East Coast teams traveling west for multiple games. Theory: Body clocks struggle, especially early games that feel like afternoon.

*Application to Props:*
- Game 1 of West Coast trip: Unders on East Coast team's stars
- By Game 3-4, they've adjusted
- Late tips (10pm ET) especially brutal for East Coast bodies

**THE ALTITUDE FACTOR**
Denver (5,280 ft) and Utah (4,226 ft) create real physiological effects. Theory: Visiting teams tire faster, especially in second half.

*Application to Props:*
- Second-half player props favor home team
- Visiting stars might hit first half numbers but fade
- High-minute players struggle most

**THE BACK-TO-BACK INVESTIGATION**
Second game of a back-to-back, especially with travel.

*Investigate for Props:*
- How does THIS player perform on B2Bs? What does their actual data show?
- What's THIS player's minutes differential on B2Bs vs rest?
- Does THIS player's efficiency or volume drop more on short rest?

**THE "4 IN 5 NIGHTS" FATIGUE INVESTIGATION**
Four games in five nights is a heavy load.

*Investigate for Props:*
- What's THIS team's full schedule context, not just "B2B or not"?
- How has THIS specific player performed in similar schedule crunches this season?
- Third and fourth games in stretch are danger zones

**THE TIME ZONE THEORY**
Teams crossing multiple time zones struggle. Most pronounced: West Coast team playing early East Coast game.

*Application to Props:*
- 1pm ET tips for West Coast teams (feels like 10am) = rough
- Bodies are literally not awake
- First quarter/first half unders on West Coast visitors

---

### MOTIVATION & PSYCHOLOGY THEORIES

**THE REVENGE GAME SKEPTICISM**
Media loves "revenge narratives" but data is mixed on whether it actually matters.

*Sharp Take:*
- Revenge is priced in by public (line is shaded)
- If anything, fade the revenge narrative
- Exception: If revenge = actual scheme adjustment (coach returning to old team with insider knowledge), that's different

**THE CONTRACT YEAR THEORY**
Players in contract years supposedly play harder/better. Some data supports this for certain positions.

*Application to Props:*
- More relevant for full-season totals than single games
- But contract year players might push through minor injuries
- Consider when player is "auditioning" vs coasting

**THE "PROVE IT" GAME**
Player facing former team, player benched recently, player called out by media/coach.

*Sharp Take:*
- Motivation is real but hard to quantify
- Usually already priced in if it's a known narrative
- Better to look for HIDDEN motivation (personal vendetta not public)

**THE EWING THEORY**
Named after Patrick Ewing—teams sometimes play BETTER without their star. Theory: Role players step up, play more freely, opponents don't prepare the same.

*Application to Props:*
- Star out → Don't just blindly bet backup's over
- Ask: Does the TEAM play better without the star?
- Sometimes stars return and team gets WORSE short-term (readjustment)

---

### MARKET BEHAVIOR THEORIES

**THE "STALE LINE" THEORY**
Some books are slow to update. The line reflects old information.

*Application to Props:*
- Props are updated less frequently than spreads
- A line set Monday morning might not reflect Tuesday's practice report
- Compare the line to recent news—does the line reflect current reality?

**THE CLOSING LINE AS TRUTH**
Sharp consensus: The closing line is the most accurate estimate of true probability.

*Application:*
- The line right before tip-off reflects the most information
- If you bet early, you're betting that your information is better than what will come later
- After the game, compare your bet to the closing line—did you have the better number?

**THE "TOO GOOD TO BE TRUE" INSTINCT**
When a line looks obviously wrong, ask why.

*Application to Props:*
- If you see "easy value," the book probably sees something you don't
- Books don't give away money—investigate why the line is where it is
- Sometimes it IS a mistake. But assume it isn't until you prove otherwise.

---

### PERFORMANCE PATTERN THEORIES

**THE HOT HAND DEBATE**
Controversial: Is "heat" real or just randomness we pattern-match?

*Sharp Take:*
- Recent research suggests hot hand IS real but smaller than people think
- Problem: By the time you notice a hot streak, line has adjusted
- Better to look for players DUE for regression than chase heat

**THE MINUTES CLIFF**
Players have efficiency drop-offs at certain minute thresholds. Often around 34-36 minutes.

*Application to Props:*
- If a player is projected for 38+ minutes, efficiency might suffer
- Volume up but percentages down = net wash
- High-minute props need efficiency maintenance, not just opportunity

**THE GARBAGE TIME DISTORTION**
Stats accumulated in blowouts aren't "real" production.

*Application to Props:*
- Player averaging 18 PPG but 4 PPG come in garbage time
- True competitive production is 14 PPG
- Especially relevant for bench players who only play in blowouts

**THE USAGE SPIKE SUSTAINABILITY**
When a player's usage suddenly jumps (teammate injury), can they maintain it?

*Sharp Take:*
- First game with new usage = often inflated (adrenaline)
- Games 2-5 = regression to realistic level
- Games 6+ = new baseline established
- The Murray "16.5 APG without Jokic" claim needs sample size check

**THE PACE POSSESSIONS THEORY**
Everything flows from possessions. More possessions = more everything.

*Application:*
- High pace game = volume boost for everyone
- Calculate possessions: Your team's pace + opponent's pace creates game pace
- But: More possessions also means more opportunities for OPPONENT
- Overs hit more in high-pace, but so do opponent props

### SPORT-SPECIFIC THEORIES

**NBA: THE FOURTH QUARTER STARS**
Some players elevate in clutch time, some disappear.

*Application:*
- Known closers (established stars) get extra usage late
- Line might not account for clutch usage boost
- But also: Close games = more free throws, clock stoppages = more possessions in same time

**NBA: THE "PLAYOFF MODE" SWITCH**
Theory: Regular season effort ≠ playoff effort. Some players coast, some don't know how to switch.

*Application:*
- Mid-season props on known "coasters" → Consider unders
- But: Load management makes this murkier—are they coasting or just being rested?

**NFL: THE SCRIPT DEPENDENCY**
Game script dominates NFL props more than any other sport.

*Application:*
- RB props die when trailing (team abandons run)
- WR props die when leading big (team runs out clock)
- QB props depend entirely on game flow
- Always model: "What happens if they're up 14?" and "What happens if they're down 14?"

**NFL: THE WEATHER WINDOWS**
Specific conditions create specific edges.

*Sharp Take:*
- Wind 15+ mph: Passing yards crater, rushing yards boom
- Rain: Passing efficiency drops but attempts might stay same
- Cold: Ball is harder to grip, drops increase
- Dome teams playing outdoors in December = disaster

**NHL: THE GOALIE CAROUSEL**
Goalie changes affect everything but are announced late.

*Application:*
- Backup goalie = more goals expected for opponent
- But ALSO: Team might play tighter defense in front of backup
- Best play is often opponent props, not own team

**NHL: THE "GETTING PUCKS ON NET" FALLACY**
Coaches always say "get pucks on net" but SOG ≠ quality chances.

*Application:*
- SOG props are high variance
- A player can have 8 SOG and 0 goals or 2 SOG and 2 goals
- If betting SOG, focus on players who shoot from everywhere (not just snipers)

### BANKROLL & PROCESS THEORIES

**THE KELLY CRITERION**
Optimal bet sizing based on edge and bankroll. Formula: (bp - q) / b

Where:
- b = odds received (decimal - 1)
- p = probability of winning
- q = probability of losing (1 - p)

*Sharp Take:*
- Most bettors use "fractional Kelly" (1/4 or 1/2 Kelly)
- Full Kelly is too aggressive for most bankrolls
- Key insight: Bet size should scale with EDGE, not confidence

**THE 1% RULE**
Never risk more than 1-2% of bankroll on a single bet.

*Application:*
- Even your "locks" should be 2% max
- Props have higher variance than spreads—adjust down
- Surviving variance is more important than maximizing winners

**THE "FLAT BETTING" DISCIPLINE**
Bet the same amount every time, regardless of confidence.

*Sharp Take:*
- Removes emotion from sizing
- Forces discipline
- If your handicapping is good, flat betting still profits
- Variable sizing only helps if your confidence calibration is ALSO good (usually it isn't)

**THE "CHASE" DEATH SPIRAL**
Increasing bets to recover losses. Classic way to blow a bankroll.

*Application:*
- Each prop is independent
- Today's losses don't make tomorrow's edges bigger
- Stick to unit sizes regardless of recent results

---

### THE JUICE AS INFORMATION

The juice (vig) on each side of a prop tells you something.

**THE JUICE TELL:**
- Heavy juice one direction (-130 vs -110) = book nervous about that side
- They're charging more because they expect action there
- Or they believe one side is more likely

**THE ROUND NUMBER TELL:**
- Lines at exactly 20.0, 25.0, 30.0 might be "placeholder" lines
- Sharp books use .5 lines more often
- Round numbers can indicate less precision in line-setting

## SECTION 10: UNCONVENTIONAL THEORIES & PROBABILITY PHILOSOPHY

These aren't mainstream betting concepts. They're frameworks from physics, philosophy, and pattern recognition that some bettors use to think about uncertainty differently. Take what resonates, leave what doesn't.

---

### THE QUANTUM PROBABILITY FRAMEWORK

**THE SUPERPOSITION PRINCIPLE**
In quantum mechanics, particles exist in multiple states simultaneously until observed. The act of measurement "collapses" the probability wave into one outcome.

*Application to Betting:*
- Before tip-off, ALL outcomes exist as real possibilities
- The game hasn't "decided" yet—every possibility is genuinely there
- Your job isn't to predict THE future, but to assess which futures are more probable
- Don't think "will he hit the over?" Think "in what percentage of possible universes does he hit?"

**THE OBSERVER EFFECT**
Observation changes the thing being observed.

*Possible Application:*
- Heavy betting attention on a prop might affect how it plays out
- When millions watch and bet on a star, does the pressure change behavior?
- National TV games have different energy than Tuesday night games
- The "big game" effect isn't just narrative—it might be physics

**THE MANY-WORLDS INTERPRETATION**
Every quantum measurement splits reality into parallel branches where each outcome occurs.

*Mental Model for Betting:*
- You're not trying to be "right"—you're trying to bet on the branch that occurs most often
- In 100 parallel universes where tonight happens, how many see the over hit?
- This removes the emotional attachment to single outcomes
- You bet the same way across all universes; some you win, some you lose

**THE UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE**
You cannot simultaneously know certain paired quantities with perfect precision.

*Application to Props:*
- The more precisely you predict volume (minutes, touches), the less certain you are about efficiency
- The more precisely you predict efficiency, the less certain about volume
- High-minute games have efficiency uncertainty; high-efficiency predictions have volume uncertainty
- Accept that perfect prediction is impossible—bet probabilities, not certainties

---

### NON-LINEAR TIME & PROBABILITY

**THE BLOCK UNIVERSE THEORY**
Physics suggests time doesn't "flow"—past, present, and future all exist simultaneously. We just experience them sequentially.

*Mental Model:*
- The outcome already "exists" in some sense
- Your bet isn't causing anything—you're aligning with probabilities
- Removes the anxiety of "making it happen"
- Focus on process, not outcome attachment

**THE PROBABILITY WAVE**
Before observation, outcomes exist as probability distributions, not fixed points.

*Application:*
- A player's "true talent" isn't a single number—it's a distribution
- Tonight's performance is a sample from that distribution
- The line represents the book's estimate of the median of that distribution
- Your edge is knowing the shape of the distribution better than the book

**ERGODICITY & THE ENSEMBLE**
A system is ergodic if a single entity over time behaves the same as many entities at one moment.

*Application to Betting:*
- One bettor betting 1,000 times ≠ 1,000 bettors betting once
- YOUR sequence of bets matters (bankroll can go to zero mid-journey)
- Even with positive expected value, you can go broke via variance
- This is why bankroll management isn't optional—it's physics

---

### CHAOS THEORY & SPORTS

**THE BUTTERFLY EFFECT**
In chaotic systems, tiny changes in initial conditions lead to vastly different outcomes.

*Application:*
- A slightly different bounce, a toe on the line, a gust of wind
- Games are chaotic systems—outcomes are sensitive to tiny variations
- This is why even "locks" lose and why variance is inescapable
- Don't mistake randomness for being wrong

**STRANGE ATTRACTORS**
Chaotic systems aren't purely random—they orbit around patterns called "strange attractors."

*Application to Props:*
- A player's performance isn't random—it orbits around their talent level
- But within that orbit, there's unpredictable variation
- Your job is to identify the attractor (true talent), not predict the variation
- Lines set on the attractor = no edge. Lines off the attractor = edge.

**SENSITIVE DEPENDENCE ON INITIAL CONDITIONS**
The state at game start heavily influences the trajectory.

*Application:*
- Early foul trouble, early deficit, early injury → entire game changes
- First quarter/period can determine whether props hit
- Props with early variance (first half, first period) are higher risk
- Full-game props have more time to regress to the attractor

### SYNCHRONICITY & PATTERN RECOGNITION

**JUNGIAN SYNCHRONICITY**
Carl Jung proposed that meaningful coincidences occur more often than chance would suggest. Patterns emerge that connect seemingly unrelated events.

*Application (Use Carefully):*
- When multiple unrelated factors align on one side → pay attention
- Injury + revenge game + altitude + B2B all pointing same direction
- Might be coincidence. Might be the universe telling you something.
- Don't bet on synchronicity alone, but notice when it reinforces your analysis

**APOPHENIA VS PATTERN RECOGNITION**
Apophenia is seeing patterns that don't exist. Pattern recognition is seeing patterns that do.

*The Challenge:*
- Human brains are pattern-matching machines—often too good
- We see streaks and slumps that are just randomness
- But SOME patterns are real (fatigue, matchup advantages, role changes)
- The skill is knowing which is which

**THE GESTALT PRINCIPLE**
The whole is greater than the sum of its parts.

*Application:*
- Don't just add up individual factors
- Ask: "What's the GESTALT of this situation?"
- Sometimes a situation just "feels" right or wrong
- Experienced bettors develop intuition that integrates information subconsciously
- Don't ignore that feeling—but don't bet on it alone either

### SUPERSTITIONS & THE COLLECTIVE UNCONSCIOUS

**THE SPORTS ILLUSTRATED COVER CURSE**
Theory: Players/teams featured on SI covers often underperform shortly after.

*Possible Explanations:*
- Regression to mean (they're on the cover BECAUSE of a hot streak)
- Distraction from media attention
- Opposing teams raise their game
- Or: Just coincidence that became self-fulfilling

*Application:*
- When a player gets heavy media attention, consider regression
- The spotlight itself might be a factor
- Public overbets the "hot" player, creating value on the under

**FULL MOON EFFECTS**
Some studies suggest human behavior changes around full moons. Most show no effect, but some show small correlations with aggression, hospital admissions, etc.

*Application (Speculative):*
- If you believe in it, check the moon phase on high-aggression sports (hockey, football)
- Probably noise. But the data isn't conclusive either way.
- At minimum, it's a reminder that factors we don't track might matter

**THE ANNOUNCER JINX**
"He hasn't missed a free throw all game..." *brick*

*The Theory:*
- Drawing attention to a streak invites its end
- Self-fulfilling through pressure/awareness
- Or: Just regression to mean happening at that moment

*Application:*
- When the narrative is about a streak, consider the streak ending
- Not because of magic—because regression is coming anyway
- The announcer jinx is just regression with good timing

**JERSEY NUMBER PATTERNS**
Some bettors track outcomes by jersey numbers, birthdates, or other seemingly arbitrary factors.

*Sharp Take:*
- Almost certainly noise
- But: If a book prices based on "obvious" factors only, any edge (even weird ones) could matter
- The point isn't that jersey numbers matter—it's that the market might miss something
- Stay open to unconventional edges, but demand evidence

### THE LUCK VS VARIANCE DEBATE

**IS LUCK REAL?**
Philosophically contested. Is "luck" just our word for variance we don't understand?

*Two Views:*

**View 1: Luck is Real**
- Some people/teams have "luck" as a persistent trait
- Bounces, calls, injuries tend to favor certain entities
- If it persists beyond expected variance, maybe it's real

**View 2: Luck is Misunderstood Variance**
- What we call "luck" is just the random component we can't predict
- Sufficient sample size eliminates "luck"
- Anyone who seems "lucky" is either skilled or hasn't played enough games

*Application:*
- Don't bet on luck as a factor
- But DO track "luck indicators" (shooting percentage vs expected, close game record vs Pythagorean)
- Teams/players running hot on luck indicators might regress

**THE GAMBLER'S FALLACY VS REGRESSION TO MEAN**
These are OPPOSITES but often confused.

**Gambler's Fallacy (WRONG):**
- "He's missed 5 in a row, he's DUE to hit"
- Past outcomes don't change future probability
- Each event is independent

**Regression to Mean (CORRECT):**
- "He's shooting 65% over 3 games but his career average is 45%—expect regression"
- Extreme performances tend to be followed by less extreme ones
- Not because of "balance" but because extremes are rare by definition

*Application:*
- Don't bet on someone being "due"
- DO bet on regression when recent performance is unsustainably extreme
- The difference: "due" is about the next shot; regression is about the sample moving toward true talent

### CONSCIOUSNESS & COLLECTIVE ENERGY

**THE HOME COURT ADVANTAGE MYSTERY**
Home teams win more. We attribute it to travel, familiarity, referees. But the effect persists even controlling for those.

*Alternative Theory:*
- 20,000 people willing the same outcome creates... something
- Collective intention might have a real effect
- Or: The players BELIEVE it does, which makes it real

*Application:*
- Don't dismiss home court as "just familiarity"
- Big crowds, playoff atmospheres, hostile environments matter
- The ENERGY of a situation might be as real as the X's and O's

**THE WILL TO WIN**
Some players "want it more." Is that measurable?

*Application:*
- Probably not directly measurable
- But: Clutch performance, desperation, and motivation are real
- The player's BELIEF that they'll win might affect the probability
- Or it doesn't, and we're just pattern-matching after the fact

**MOMENTUM AS PHYSICS VS PSYCHOLOGY**
In physics, momentum is mass × velocity. In sports, momentum is... vibes?

*The Question:*
- Is "momentum" real, or is it just us narrating randomness?
- Some research shows momentum effects; other research shows none
- At minimum, players BELIEVE in momentum, which might make it real

*Application:*
- Don't bet on momentum alone
- But: Game state (blowout vs close) affects player behavior
- A team "with momentum" might play differently than one "reeling"
- Whether momentum is real or perceived, it affects actions

### THE META-AWARENESS FRAMEWORK

**THINKING ABOUT THINKING**
The best bettors are aware of their own cognitive processes.

*Questions to Ask:*
- Am I falling for a narrative?
- Is this confirmation bias?
- Am I overconfident because I want to be right?
- Would I bet this if it were on a team I'd never heard of?

**THE MAP IS NOT THE TERRITORY**
Your model of reality is not reality itself.

*Application:*
- Statistics are maps, not the territory
- The game doesn't know about your model
- When reality diverges from your model, update the model—don't insist reality is wrong

**SECOND-ORDER THINKING**
First-order: "He averages 25, line is 23.5, bet the over"
Second-order: "Everyone sees that, so the line is shaded—is there still value?"
Third-order: "But if everyone fades the obvious, maybe the original bet is right"

*Application:*
- Always go at least one level deeper
- What does the market see? What does the market think the market sees?
- The edge is in seeing something the market doesn't, not in seeing what everyone sees

These don't replace analysis. They expand how you THINK about analysis.
